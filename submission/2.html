Initial questions How does Incubation Period relate to Clutch Size in Birds?
Description:  This scatterplot is an examination of the relationship between the Incubation Period in days to the number of clutches per year in birds. I found this interesting as growing up I always had birds around me since my uncle had an aviary and I never considered how different birds take longer to mature and there was always less of them so hopefully this can provide some insight. What I was really interested in was if the number of days for incubation would effect the number of clutches significantly, For instance I was hoping that the less amount of time in incubation the more clutches there would be. And that both does and doesn't look like the case.
Insight: We can see that there is a significant amount of banding in the visualization. The banding occurs around each half year mark on the clutches per year axis, starting on 1 per year. This would suggest that Birds lay eggs on a very predictable time scale.and throughout all species that they dont differ too much as the majority of species have 1 or 2 clutches of eggs per year.

There is some suggestion that if a species has a short incubation period that it has many clutches due to the data points at the top left of the graph. However this seems more like an exception because we can clearly see that in the species that only lay 1 clutch a year (the bottom band) that there are plenty of species with short incubation periods. In general, the visualization shows that the majority of bird species don't differ very much in number of clutches and incubation time.

There is of course also an outlier The Califonia Condor But this is the most extreme outlier in the data. It is a bird that is able to lay two clutches per year and thoe clutches take a significant amount of Time to incubate

From the visualization I would be hesitant to suggest that there is a correlation between incubation time and number of clutches per year in birds.

Design considerations I have used a scatterplot as it is a very effective way to show the relationship between two quantitative variables, taking advantage of our ability to accurately perceive variation in both horizontal and vertical position.

I considered using just a bar chart and representing the average incubation time for each band of clutches per year. Bur this proved to not show much at all and made the data not very useful.

I also considered using a bubble chart to represent the average incubation time as the label and the size of the bubble would be the count of the number of species in the clutch per year. In testing this would've done a good job at showing that as clutch size increases incubation time decreases but unfortunately it was bias.

At the end I stand by the decision that a scatter plot was the bes way to show off this data even if it showed us that we cant really answer the question I posed.
